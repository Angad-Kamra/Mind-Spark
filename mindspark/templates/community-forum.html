Hey fellow ADHD gamers!

I've been trying to figure out which games actually work best with our brains instead of against them. We all know our brains are constantly seeking that dopamine hit, and some games are WAY better at providing consistent rewards than others.

I've noticed that games with these features tend to work best for me:

  • Quick feedback loops (getting rewards every few minutes)
  • Visual/audio cues for achievements
  • Progress tracking that's easy to see
  • Multiple short-term goals rather than one big goal

My current favorites are:

1. Rogue Legacy 2 - Each run is quick, you always make progress even when you die, and the upgrades give constant dopamine hits

2. Hades - Similar reasons, plus the story progresses in small chunks that keep me engaged

3. Animal Crossing - Daily rewards and tasks that are perfect for short attention spans

What games do you find work best with your ADHD brain? And why do you think they work so well?

Great topic, DopamineHunter! 👍

I've actually been keeping a journal about this very subject. For me, games that allow for "hyperfocus sessions" but don't punish you for walking away work amazingly well.

I've noticed that games with these features tend to work best for me:
- Quick feedback loops (getting rewards every few minutes)
- Visual/audio cues for achievements

These are absolutely critical! I'd add one more: games that have adjustable difficulty that can match your current focus level. Some days I can handle complex strategy, other days I need something more forgiving.

My top picks:

1. Slay the Spire - Card-based roguelike where each battle is a small, satisfying puzzle. I can play one fight then take a break if needed.

2. Satisfactory - I know this seems counterintuitive as it's a massive factory game, but it lets me focus on small, discrete projects and the automation is incredibly satisfying.

3. Forza Horizon - I can race for 3 minutes or 3 hours, and the constant stream of rewards keeps me engaged without feeling grindy.

Also, I use the Pomodoro technique with my gaming - 25 minutes of focused play, then a 5-minute break. Works wonders for avoiding burnout!

Just jumping in with a different perspective!

While I agree with everything said about quick rewards and feedback loops, I've found that some longer, more immersive games can work amazingly well for ADHD - specifically open-world RPGs that let you constantly switch between different activities.

For example, in Skyrim or The Witcher 3, I can bounce between main quests, side quests, crafting, exploration, combat, etc. whenever I start getting bored with one activity. This actually works WITH my tendency to get distracted rather than against it!

The key is finding games that don't force you down a linear path, but instead offer multiple valid ways to progress at any given moment.

Another type that works great for me: building/creation games like Minecraft or Stardew Valley that let me hyperfocus on projects I choose myself.

Has anyone else noticed this pattern? Games that accommodate task-switching rather than punishing it?

Mom of two ADHD gamers here. I've been following this thread with interest because I'm always looking for games that will work well for my kids (11 and 14).

@RapidGamer - We use a visual timer at home! It's a simple countdown timer with a colored disk that disappears as time runs out. Much more effective than a digital timer for ADHD brains.

This is such a fantastic discussion! As an ADHD coach who uses gaming as part of my coaching toolkit, I'm loving all these insights.

@DopamineHunter - You're absolutely right about those reward systems. Our brains are literally seeking that dopamine, and game designers who understand this (intentionally or not) create experiences that work better for us.

@GameMaster99 - Your point about games that accommodate task-switching is spot on! I call this the "playground approach" - giving multiple activities within the same space so we can follow our interest shifts without having to completely disengage.

@RapidGamer - Time blindness is such a common challenge. In addition to timers, I recommend the "bookending" technique: decide BEFORE you start gaming both when you'll stop AND what you'll do next. Creating that next anchor helps our brains transition.

One more category I'd add to this discussion: puzzle games with infinite undos like Baba Is You, Stephen's Sausage Roll, or A Monster's Expedition. They allow for experimentation without punishment, which works well with our tendency to try multiple approaches rapidly.

For anyone struggling with gaming addiction (which can be especially challenging with ADHD), I recommend checking out the "Healthy Gaming" section on this forum. It's important to distinguish between games that work well with our brains versus those that exploit our vulnerabilities.
